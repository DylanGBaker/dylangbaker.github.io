The Internet, Society and Design Justice

A large part of modern society pretty much lives on the internet. And when you have a lot of people using something there is bound to be a large diversity in that society. You would think that logically all you have to do is design something accessible for all the different types of people that exist out there because people want to help people and be ethical in doing so, right? Unfortunately, that’s not always the case.

Of course, there are people out there that design to be accessible to everyone that uses that product and mistakes are bound to happen as not everybody is perfect. The issues arise when there is a lack of responsibility taken as well as poor design choices and mentality.

Design Injustice

Let’s start by looking at an example of design injustice. Some airports have a machine called a millimeter wave scanner which checks to see if an individual may be carrying something on them that is illegal. The machine uses gender to determine the outcome and the issue is that it only has the choice of male or female. This becomes a problem for people who don’t identify as either of those. This then causes problems with the airport security and just causes an unwanted amount of stress and issues that can just be avoided through accessible design. [1]

You would think ok then, let’s just update the software and then we are good to go. Sure, that does help in the future, but it does not address the issue as to why this problem occurred and how it can be prevented in the future. That’s the point of mistakes, to learn from them and then prevent it from happening in the future. When making software people should try and stick to the ACM code of ethics as closely as possible. The designers of the millimeter wave scanner failed in a couple areas of ethics such as “avoid harm” as well as “be fair and take action not to discriminate” to name a few [4].

So, what can be done to prevent these mistakes from happening?

In “Algorithmic Injustices: Towards a Relational Ethics” , it mentions that people who are not thought of in the design of stuff that society uses are often the ones most affected [2]. Take the millimeter wave scanner for example. For Sasha Costanza-Chock this was design injustice as the machine determined that they were a risk. This causes a security protocol to then take place which is also cis-normative and binary and therefore another process is not accessible to others who are not male or female [1].

to others who are not male or female [1]. The machines software ultimately showed the injustice as the different security systems in place were only accessible to male or female and not inclusive of people who identify differently.

We ultimately need to design with not just the majority in mind but all people that our software could affect. Using the machine as an example again, it deals with people. So, when designing those systems, you need to think of any possible situation that could arise with people’s genders. Since there are a lot of people on this earth the system was bound to see a transgendered person, yet the software did not have an option for that. I feel like the company who made the machine can implement different gender options yet there was not, and I feel like that was just ignorance on their part and maybe just assumed that the airport security can deal with it. So, in my eyes we need to stray away from these thought patterns that if the problem arises then we can just quickly fix it and then continue on without thinking of the repercussions.

So how do we prevent this? We need to stop thinking of just the majority of software and internet users. In our design choices we need to ask ourselves hard questions that could even make us uncomfortable as we may not know the answers to those questions at that moment however, by asking those questions we start reducing the injustice in our designs. Of course, this then needs to be shared and spoken about on the internet itself so that awareness is created.

We need to stop thinking that just because it is software it is an accident and therefore not finding the root cause of the issues. Nobody is perfect though and mistakes will most likely be made but by changing our way of thinking in terms of our design then we start to mitigate the mistakes and learning from them rather than just saying sorry and making a quick software fix. By doing this, developers should be sticking to the ACM code of ethics more closely and hence we will have better software.
